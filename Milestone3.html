We realized at this point that a significant portion of our list of followers had scores that were NaN values. Upon further examination, we determined that a NaN score is given to Twitter accounts that have never tweeted. Since a Twitter bot that does not tweet is unable to bear malice toward society, it would not be a bot worth detecting. Therefore, we decided to assume that followers with NaN scores are all humans.

 

Now, a Botometer score that is not a NaN can take on continuous values between 0 and 1, and it is a measure of how likely it is that the associated Twitter account is a bot. We decided to derive our response variable as being equal to 1 if the Botometer score is greater than 0.5, and equal to 0 otherwise. Unfortunately, we encountered an error during this process, which we found to be because of duplicate entried in the Botometer dataset. It turns out there were people who were followers of more than one of the official government accounts in our list. It was somewhat surprising to find out that Pandas couldn’t decide between the duplicates, even though they all had the same ‘bot_or_not’ value.

 

The fix for this issue turned out to be pretty simple. We removed the duplicate rows from the Botometer dataset by using the method ‘drop_duplicates()’ to get rid of the error.

3.2      Baseline Classification Model

The algorithm we apply in our baseline model is logistic regression classification. We used a C parameter of 10,000,000. The accuracy score of our baseline model is 0.907, whereas the accuracy score of predicting all zeros is 0.894. Thus, we see approximately a 1.3% improvement. Our baseline model reduces the predicting all zeros error rate of 10.6% to 9.3%, which can be seen as a significant improvement, depending on one’s perspective.

4       Future Plans and Roadmap

The following are our plans for improving upon our current baseline model.

4.1      Examine impact of including TF-IDF matrix in model features

·       TF-IDF is a method of putting weights on the words that are in the text. 

·       We were not able to run spell correction due to computational/time limits for milestone 3, but we will attempt to spell correct the data for the final report.

·       We will get TF-IDF matrices with ngrams = (1,1), (1,2), (1,3), and (1,4). We will compare these matrices to see if our model can be improved. 

 

4.2      Examine impact of including Word2Vec values in model features

·       We want to figure out a way to score each tweet by tuning the weight of each score vector of the words in the tweet.

·       We may want to see if the TF-IDF score can be used as a parameter for determining the weights of our word score vectors.

 

4.3      Explore Possibility of Utilizing Hashtags Text

·       We think that figuring out a way to use the actual hashtag names as features might improve our predictions.

·       This would require figuring out how to convert the hashtag name strings into numbers suitable for analysis.

·       Potentially, we want to see if user/tweet groups that share a common hashtag are more or less likely to be a bot.

 

4.4      Data Grouping/Aggregation

·       We believe there may be room for improvement in grouping/aggregating the tweets data by ‘user_screen_name’ and create classification models for user group data instead of on Tweets.

 

4.5      Dimensionality Reduction

·       We are planning on using TF-IDF matrices, in which case we may want to explore ways to reduce the dimensionality of our data.

 

4.6      Additional Models to Include
